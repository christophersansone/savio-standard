## 1. Executive Summary

Catholic grade schools promise a community formed by the Gospel, yet many families experience something different: social aggression that persists despite everyone's good intentions, a school office asked to carry burdens it was never built to carry, and a culture where the kindest families quietly leave. The Savio Project (a working title, pending the founding team) — a lay movement under the patronage of St. John Bosco, whose Oratory formed young people through reason, religion, and loving-kindness — is a parent-led response. It does not ask the school or parish for permission, budget, or policy change. It organizes parents directly — to build real community, adopt a shared standard of conduct, learn a common language for social aggression, handle parent-to-parent conflict with charity and skill, raise children who stand up for one another through a dedicated children's program, and form children as wise, supervised users of technology from an early age.

The strategy borrows one proven mechanic from the Wait Until 8th movement — commitments organized by school and grade level that "activate" when a critical mass of families signs on, so no family stands alone — while charting its own course on digital formation. It draws its content from the best available research on relational aggression and bystander intervention, and its spiritual frame from the Church's own teaching that parents — not schools — are the primary educators of their children (CCC 2223; *Gravissimum Educationis*).

Year one is a pilot at a single school: recruit a founding team, launch the Family Covenant, run an eight-part parent formation series, stand up School Leads and Homeroom Hosts as the community backbone, train a small group of conflict facilitators, and publish the core content library. The app follows as a delivery and coordination layer once the content and community model is proven. The Project launches as a ministry; if it gains momentum, the model, content, and app are designed to be packaged for any Catholic school community in the country.

## 2. The Problem We Are Naming

Every element of this plan traces back to six observable realities. Naming them clearly — and charitably — is the first act of the Project, because vague language ("kids will be kids," "drama") is one of the mechanisms by which the problem persists. Nothing in this section is a criticism of any school or the people who serve in it; it is a clear-eyed account of why a community cannot delegate its culture.

1. **The Golden Rule has become a goal instead of a standard.** Parents enroll expecting their children to be surrounded by peers who treat one another with kindness and dignity. In practice, basic decency is treated as an aspiration to celebrate rather than a norm to expect.

2. **Parents outsource social conflict to the school.** We do this for understandable reasons — we trust the school and assume it is best positioned to act. But the arrangement is structurally lopsided. A parent has one child to advocate for and form; an administrator must balance the parents on both sides of every conflict, the teachers and staff, the pastor, the superintendent, the bishop, donor families, and privacy laws like FERPA that often prevent them from even explaining what was done. Meanwhile, the office is inundated with social conflicts that never required disciplinary action at all — ordinary friction that parents and children, with the right skills, could resolve themselves. Outsourcing overloads the school and atrophies the very muscles in parents — direct charitable conversation, community accountability — that actually resolve these situations.

3. **The school cannot enforce its way to a culture, especially in the upper grades.** Even the most dedicated administrator faces structural headwinds. Discipline alone rarely changes social behavior — relational aggression is precisely the kind of harm that slips past rules. Confidentiality obligations mean parents rarely see what was done, so diligent action can look like inaction. The most difficult cases often involve the families least receptive to feedback, and every disciplinary decision carries ripple effects across enrollment, staff, and parish relationships that parents never see. The conclusion is not that schools fail us — it is that a school office was never built to be the primary instrument of culture change, and expecting it to be one is a category error. The adults best positioned to shape the culture are not employed by the school.

4. **Parent-to-parent conversations fail.** When one parent approaches another about a conflict between their children, the default responses are denial ("there's no way my kid did that") or reversal ("your kid deserved it"). There is no shared standard, no shared language, and no neutral process, so these conversations damage relationships instead of repairing them — and drive parents back to the school office as the only channel left.

5. **Adverse selection hollows out the community.** Taken to its logical conclusion, the kind and loving families withdraw their children, concentrating the aggressive families that remain. Every kind family that leaves makes the school worse for the kind families still there. This is the death spiral the Project exists to reverse.

6. **Phones and social media are industrializing the damage.** Smartphones and group chats expose children to pornography, cruelty, and adult content years before they can process it, extend social aggression past the school day into every bedroom, and supply new weapons (screenshots, pile-ons, exclusion made visible). The research popularized by Jonathan Haidt's *The Anxious Generation* links the phone-based childhood directly to the rise in anxiety and social cruelty this Project confronts — and no school policy can reach the family kitchen, the bedroom, or the weekend group chat where most of it happens.

None of this is unique to any one school, and none of it is fixed by better policy alone. A community of professed believers holds the two resources no administration can supply: the Gospel, and each other. The Project exists to put both to work — in partnership with the school's mission, on ground only parents can hold.

## 3. Theory of Change: Why Parents, and Why We Don't Wait for Permission

### 3.1 The theological warrant

The Church teaches that parents are the first and primary educators of their children — schools assist parents, not the reverse (Catechism of the Catholic Church 2223; Vatican II, *Gravissimum Educationis*). Organizing parents to form their children's character and protect their innocence is not a workaround of legitimate authority; it is the exercise of the most legitimate authority there is. This framing matters practically: it means the Project needs no one's approval, and it gives parents an answer when someone asks who authorized this.

### 3.2 Why culture change must come from parents

1. **Parents control the levers administration cannot touch.** Schools cannot control what happens at birthday parties, in group chats, at sports practice, or in the family kitchen where a child either learns contempt or learns charity. That is where culture is actually made.

2. **Bullying is sustained by the audience, not the bully.** Research on bystander dynamics consistently shows aggression persists because peers reward it with attention or silence. Reviews of bystander-intervention programs found nine of ten improved bystander confidence and intent, and most improved actual intervention behavior. You do not need to convert the aggressive families to change the culture — you need to convert the crowd. That is a numbers game parents can win.

3. **Critical mass beats policy.** Wait Until 8th grew from one Austin mom to 147,000+ pledged families at 58,000+ schools with zero institutional authority, because its pledge activates when ten families in the same grade at the same school sign — proof that visible, mutual commitment among a minority of parents changes what feels normal for everyone. The same mechanic applies to conduct, not just phones.

4. **Restorative, virtue-based approaches work in Catholic settings.** The Archdiocese of St. Louis's Virtue-Based Restorative Discipline reports that 86% of schools implementing with fidelity saw reductions in bullying. Its insight — form the adults first, treat mistreatment as a wound to be healed and a virtue to be built, not merely a rule broken — is the Project's pedagogical backbone, applied through parents instead of through administration.

### 3.3 The independence doctrine

The Project deliberately requires no approval from the school or parish. Institutions will likely resist at first, because parent organization reduces their control of the narrative. The posture is therefore: independent, never adversarial. The Project never publicly criticizes the school, never organizes against administrators, and always leaves the door open. The goal is that within two to three years the school asks to adopt the Project's language and programs because its families already speak them. Independence is also the growth strategy: a model that depends on an enthusiastic principal cannot scale — the model must work anywhere, whatever the local disposition, and be a gift to the school rather than a demand on it.

## 4. Vision, Mission, and Principles

**Vision.** Catholic school communities where the Golden Rule is the enforced standard of daily life — where kind families stay, aggressive behavior finds no audience, and children's innocence is guarded by a united front of parents.

**Mission.** To equip and organize Catholic school parents — independent of school administration — to build real community, adopt shared standards, master a common language for social aggression, resolve conflicts between families with charity and skill, and raise upstanders.

### Patronage and Identity

The Project is a lay movement of Catholic school families — not affiliated with the Salesian Society or any religious congregation — under the patronage of St. John Bosco and St. Dominic Savio. Bosco's Oratory in the Valdocco district of Turin was part playground, part school, part church, part home, built for young people whom the institutions of his day could not reach. His "preventive system" is the Project's method in miniature — form young people through reason, religion, and loving-kindness so thoroughly that discipline is rarely needed, rather than punishing failures after the fact — and his axiom, "It is not enough to love the young; they must know that they are loved," is the standard for every meeting, conversation, and intervention. St. Dominic Savio, Bosco's fourteen-year-old pupil who organized his classmates and defended the smallest boys from bullying, is the patron of the children's program (Pillar 5): sanctity within reach of a schoolchild, expressed as courage on behalf of others.

**On the name.** "The Savio Project" is the current working name, honoring St. Dominic Savio; the final name belongs to the founding team. Alternatives discussed include names built on Bosco's "Oratory" (The Oratory Project, The Family Oratory, The Parents' Oratory, Oratory Families) — any name should honor the patrons' model without implying Salesian affiliation. The children's program will carry its own kid-facing name, also to be chosen (one candidate already in early development: a club built on the Golden Rule).

### Principles

1. **The standard, not the goal.** Kindness and dignity are the floor. We do not celebrate the absence of cruelty; we expect it.

2. **My child is capable of both.** Every family signs up to the premise that their child can be the victim, the aggressor, or the silent crowd — often all three in the same year. This single admission disarms the "not my kid" reflex that kills parent-to-parent conversations.

3. **Charity with a spine.** We assume goodwill, speak privately and directly, and never gossip — and we do not confuse charity with conflict avoidance. Naming harm clearly is an act of love toward both children.

4. **Restoration over prosecution.** The purpose of every intervention is a repaired relationship and a changed habit, not a verdict. This is both the Gospel and what the evidence says works.

5. **Independent, never adversarial.** We need no permission and we make no enemies. We speak well of the school or not at all.

6. **Together or not at all.** Commitments activate in groups, never alone. No family should be the only one holding a line.

7. **Confidentiality is sacred.** The Project is structurally protected from becoming a gossip network. What is shared in a facilitated conversation stays there. Children are never named in any written or digital record.

## 5. Program Architecture: The Six Pillars

Everything the Project builds — content, events, and eventually the app — hangs on six pillars. Each pillar answers one of the six named problems.

| Pillar | What it is | Problem it answers |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Community | School Leads and Homeroom Hosts — named volunteer roles serving the whole school and every homeroom; the relational fabric that makes everything else possible | Isolation; adverse selection of kind families |
| 2. Formation | Parent education series on social aggression, child development, and digital childhood | Outsourcing to an overloaded school office |
| 3. Shared Language & Standards | The Family Covenant plus a common vocabulary taught to parents and children alike | No shared standard; "kids will be kids" vagueness |
| 4. Conflict Navigation | A parent-to-parent protocol and trained volunteer facilitators | Failed parent conversations; denial and script-flipping |
| 5. Upstander Kids | The children's program (name TBD): monthly club meetings with skits and education, mentoring, and a companion learning app | Aggression sustained by silent bystanders |
| 6. Digital Oversight | Shared, non-technical household norms — message review, no bedroom devices, age-appropriate guardrails — plus pointers to recommended tools | Phones and social media crushing innocence |

### Pillar 1 — Community

Culture cannot be built among strangers, and a parent will not receive hard feedback from someone they have never shared a meal with. Two facts of Catholic grade school life make community unusually buildable — and unusually neglected. First, the grade: largely the same families travel together for nine years, the majority of their children's formative life. Relationships do form through sports and other extracurriculars, but those self-select, drift cliquey, and always leave families on the outskirts. Second, the homeroom: the room a child is placed in is effectively their entire social world for the school year, and rooms reshuffle annually — so by eighth grade nearly every family will have shared a homeroom with nearly every other. The Project builds on these two rhythms rather than inventing a new one, through two named volunteer roles:

**School Leads — a small school-wide team.** Three or four families who champion the movement at their school: they run the school-wide programming (kickoff night, the formation series logistics, the two or three all-school family socials), recruit and support the Homeroom Hosts each fall as rooms are announced, and hold the covenant mechanics for the school. At the pilot, the founding team fills these roles; as the Project spreads, each school's Leads are its local leadership.

**Homeroom Hosts — two to three families per homeroom, re-formed each year.** The ground-level unit, and where the culture actually lives. Because homerooms are capped around twenty to thirty children, two or three host families per room scales at any school size. The Hosts' work is maintaining the Savio culture among the room's parents and children: keeping the covenant's standard and shared language present, making sure every family is known and included, welcoming newcomers, noticing early signs of trouble while they're small, and connecting the parents whose children are in daily contact that year — exactly the relationships needed if a conflict later surfaces between their kids. In service of that, Hosts coordinate a simple gathering for the room's families near the start of the year and at least one more in the second half, with every family invited every time. Because rooms re-mix annually, the role rebuilds the web of relationships year after year, and since the same families travel together for nine years, by eighth grade nearly every family has been hosted alongside nearly every other.

The critical design rule: **roles are bounded; membership never is.** There is no group to join and no roster to be left off. Every gathering is open to the entire homeroom or school, invitations always go to all, and the named roles exist to serve the whole unit — a Lead or Host who convenes an exclusive subset has misunderstood the job, and the written role descriptions say so. This is the structural answer to the clique problem: structure lives in the roles, openness lives in the events. Complementary elements: a dads' group and a moms' group (aggression dynamics and parental blind spots differ by sex, and single-sex groups produce more honest conversation). Inclusion is not a committee; it is the first duty written into every role.

### Pillar 2 — Formation

Parents cannot enforce a standard they cannot articulate, and most parents have never been taught the mechanics of relational aggression, the difference between conflict and bullying, or what actually changes a child's behavior. The formation program is an eight-session annual series (Section 9) plus an evergreen content library (Section 10), drawing on the relational-aggression literature (Simmons, Wiseman, Crick), bystander research, Virtue-Based Restorative Discipline, and *The Anxious Generation* — all framed in Catholic anthropology: virtue, conscience, the dignity of the person, sin and mercy.

### Pillar 3 — Shared Language & Standards

The Covenant (Section 6) is the standard; the Glossary (Section 7) is the language. Both are taught to parents and children so that a fourth grader, her parents, and the parents of the girl who excluded her are all using the same words with the same meanings. Shared language is what converts "drama" — a word that hides everything — into specific, discussable behavior.

### Pillar 4 — Conflict Navigation

The Project's most distinctive offering: a defined protocol for parent-to-parent conversations (Section 8) and a bench of three to five trained volunteer facilitators — respected parents, ideally with counseling, HR, mediation, or pastoral backgrounds — who can host a structured two-family conversation when direct contact fails. This replaces the current binary of "awkward confrontation in the parking lot" or "email the principal and hope."

### Pillar 5 — Upstander Kids: The Children's Program

Children ultimately police their own culture, and the Project's children's program — name to be chosen by the founding team, with St. Dominic Savio as patron — trains them to do it. It is the kid-facing version of the whole movement: monthly club meetings built around skits, stories, games, and short lessons that teach the shared language, the difference between conflict and bullying, and practiced upstander responses. Meetings run in age tiers (K–2: kindness habits and telling vs. tattling; 3–5: naming exclusion and rumor-spreading, simple intervention scripts; 6–8: relational aggression, group-chat dynamics, the moral weight of the bystander) and are led by parent volunteers outside school hours — homes, parish hall if available, park pavilions if not.

Skits are the signature method. Children rehearse the exact moments — the lunch table that closes, the group chat that turns, the "you can't sit with us" — until the upstander response is muscle memory rather than a poster on a wall, and older tiers performing for younger ones become teachers of the standard. A mentor program (Savio's own model: he did precisely this at his school) pairs trained seventh- and eighth-graders with younger grades, converting the top of the school's social hierarchy into its guardians. A learning-management-style companion app, already in early development, delivers the curriculum, tracks milestones and badges between meetings, and gives families a shared track to follow at home (Section 11).

### Pillar 6 — Digital Oversight

The Project rejects both failure modes: unrestricted early access, and total prohibition that ends with handing a thirteen-year-old an unfiltered phone and no training. The middle path is an apprenticeship — begun early, with age-appropriate guardrails that loosen as responsibility is demonstrated. But the Project's teaching keeps its emphasis where parents have the most leverage and need the least technical skill: oversight. The covenant norms are simple, non-technical, and enforceable by any household: parents may review their child's messages at any time, and the child knows it; devices never sleep in bedrooms; access is age-appropriate and filtered; and no child receives unfiltered, unsupervised access at any age. Families who prefer a fuller delay (such as the Wait Until 8th pledge) are welcome and fully compatible — the covenant imposes norms, not a timeline. Adopted grade-wide, these norms dissolve both the "everyone else has one" pressure and the more corrosive "everyone else's is unfiltered."

The Project offers general resources — a short oversight guide for parents, guardrail concepts by age, and pointers to recommended tools — and the formation series devotes a session to the subject (Section 9). The technical enforcement layer (device controls, filtering, staged profiles) is deliberately out of scope for the Project's own content: it is deep, fast-moving, and better served by dedicated products that can keep pace with the platforms. The founding team anticipates recommending a companion product — separately built and always disclosed as such — that enforces these norms and dovetails with the Project's teaching. Hands-on workshops may supplement the pilot, but they are not the scalable path. The Project's job is to make the norms normal; a tool's job is to make them easy.

## 6. The Family Covenant

The Covenant is the Project's central artifact — one page, signed as a household, renewed each school year. Full text: [content/covenant.md](../content/covenant.md).

Its eight commitments in brief: the Golden Rule as standard, not aspiration; humility (my child is capable of harm, and I will listen and thank the parent who tells me); directness (concerns go privately to the family, never to the rumor mill); teaching children to include and stand up; accountability and repair at home; digital oversight; presence at community gatherings; and prayer for the school's children.

Like the Wait Until 8th pledge, it activates by grade: when eight families in a grade sign, all signers are notified and introduced to one another; until then, signatures remain private, so no family ever stands alone. Design notes: the covenant deliberately contains nothing the school could object to, nothing that names or targets anyone, and nothing enforceable by anything except conscience and community — which is the point. The wording of commitment 2 is the heart of the document; it is the pre-commitment that makes the Parent-to-Parent Protocol (Section 8) work.

## 7. Shared Language: A Starter Glossary

Taught to parents in session two of the formation series and to children in the children's program meetings. The definitions below are the working core; the full glossary is a content-library document.

**Conflict vs. bullying.** Conflict is a disagreement between rough equals and is a normal, even necessary part of childhood — children must learn to resolve it themselves. Bullying is repeated aggression with a power imbalance and intent to harm. The distinction matters: over-labeling conflict as bullying teaches fragility; under-labeling bullying as conflict abandons the victim.

**Relational aggression.** Harm inflicted through relationships rather than fists: exclusion, rumor-spreading, alliance-building against a target, the silent treatment, conditional friendship. The dominant form of aggression in grades 3–8, especially among girls, and the form schools are worst at addressing (Crick; Simmons, *Odd Girl Out*; Wiseman, *Queen Bees and Wannabes*).

**Social exclusion.** The deliberate, visible leaving-out of a child — the party everyone but one was invited to, the lunch table that closes. Distinct from mere preference; the signature is that the exclusion is meant to be seen.

**Bystander / upstander.** The bystander watches or stays silent, and their attention is the aggressor's paycheck. The upstander intervenes, defuses, refuses to laugh, includes the target, or gets adult help. Converting bystanders to upstanders is the highest-leverage cultural intervention known to the research.

**Flipping the script.** The reversal maneuver: when confronted, recasting the aggressor as the real victim ("your kid deserved it," "she started it"). Related to the clinical pattern DARVO — Deny, Attack, Reverse Victim and Offender. Naming this pattern in advance is the best inoculation against it.

**Teasing vs. taunting.** Teasing is mutual, affectionate, and stops when it stings. Taunting is one-directional, meant to sting, and escalates when it lands. Children know the difference; this language lets them say it.

**Digital pile-on.** Group-chat aggression: screenshots shared for mockery, coordinated exclusion, the 100-message thread about one absent child. Invisible to schools, fully visible to covenant parents who review chats.

**Repair.** The required response to harm: genuine apology, restitution where possible, changed behavior. The restorative alternative to both empty "say sorry" rituals and pure punishment.

## 8. The Parent-to-Parent Protocol

This is the Project's answer to the failed conversation — taught in the formation series (session 5, with live role-play), printed as a one-page card, and eventually built into the app. Full guide, with scripts and worked examples: [content/parent-to-parent-protocol.md](../content/parent-to-parent-protocol.md).

It has three modes. **Approaching** (when your child was harmed): pray first, get your child's account twice and hold it loosely, then contact the other family privately — opening with alliance ("I need your help with something between our kids, and I'd want you to come to me if it were reversed"), describing behavior rather than character, and closing with a small concrete ask and a follow-up date. **Receiving** (when your child caused harm): thank the parent for coming to you instead of around you — the sentence that is the culture change — listen without adjudicating on the spot, talk with your child, follow up when you said you would, and if your child did the harm, require repair, apply consequences at home, and close the loop. **Facilitated conversations**: when direct contact fails, either family may request a trained volunteer facilitator for a structured two-family meeting — restorative ground rules, no verdicts, written agreements, a follow-up date; a parent-level adaptation of Virtue-Based Restorative Discipline's circle practice. Facilitators receive a day of training and a written scope: they facilitate, never adjudicate, and carry nothing out of the room.

### 8.5 Escalation boundaries

The protocol handles social aggression between children of willing families. It does not replace mandatory channels: physical abuse, sexual matter, threats of self-harm or violence, or criminal behavior go to the school, the diocese's safe-environment office, and where applicable law enforcement — immediately and without a facilitated preamble. Facilitators are trained on these lines, and the written materials state them plainly. The Project also never counsels a family against reporting anything to the school; it adds a parallel channel, it never blocks one.

### 9.2 The formation series (eight sessions, monthly, 90 minutes)

1. **The State of Childhood.** The *Anxious Generation* findings, the local reality, and why parents — not the school — are the ones who can fix it. Ends with the Covenant.
2. **Conflict vs. Bullying, and the Words for Both.** The glossary session. When to coach your child through conflict and when to intervene.
3. **Relational Aggression.** The hidden curriculum of grades 3–8: exclusion, rumor, alliance, the group chat. Separate breakout discussions for parents of girls and boys.
4. **Raising an Upstander.** The bystander research, and the scripts and dinner-table habits that produce children who intervene.
5. **The Hard Conversation.** The parent-to-parent protocol, taught with live role-play. The session everyone will remember.
6. **The Digital Front.** Phones, group chats, and pornography exposure — a frank, well-prepared segment on what children are actually seeing and saying online, then the oversight norms: message review, no bedroom devices, age-appropriate guardrails, and where to find recommended tools that enforce them.
7. **When It's Your Kid.** Discovering your child is the aggressor: shame, denial, repair, and consequence at home. The least attended and most important session; consider pairing with confession availability.
8. **Building the Village.** Inclusion as a discipline, the mentor program, year-two commitments, and commissioning of next year's School Leads and Homeroom Hosts.

### 9.3 Founding team roles

The founding team doubles as the pilot school's School Leads — with one distinction kept deliberately clear even while the same people hold both kinds of role: the roles below belong to the *community* (the local, independent group of parents), while the steward and maintainers belong to the *project*. The community role structure — Director, Assistant Director, Communications Coordinator, and Events Coordinator (the leadership team), plus the Facilitator bench and Homeroom Hosts — is defined with full pitches and duties in [content/roles-and-responsibilities.md](../content/roles-and-responsibilities.md). Every volunteer position has a written description stating its duties, its term, and its first duty of inclusion. All roles are designed to be done at a few hours a week or less; the plan deliberately avoids any role that requires a heroic individual, because heroic-individual ministries die in year three.

## 10. Content Plan

Content is the asset that scales; events are the asset that bonds. Everything produced for the pilot is written once and packaged for reuse by future schools.

### 10.1 Core library (produced before or during year one)

- **The Covenant** — one page, plus a two-page rationale with the theological and research grounding.
- **The Glossary** — parent version and child versions (K–2, 3–5, 6–8).
- **The Protocol Guide** — the Section 8 material as a printable one-pager plus a longer guide with worked examples and scripts.
- **Formation series kit** — for each of the eight sessions: presenter deck, discussion guide, take-home sheet, and a recorded version for parents who miss it.
- **Children's program meeting kits** — monthly meeting plans by age tier: skit scripts, stories, games, discussion questions, and take-home cards a trained parent volunteer can run.
- **Facilitator manual** — training curriculum, ground rules, scope and escalation boundaries, case-refusal criteria.
- **The Digital Oversight guide** — the household norms, guardrail concepts by age, a family tech agreement template, and a maintained shortlist of recommended tools (any founder-built product on the list is disclosed as such).
- **Situation guides** — short, findable pieces: "My daughter was left out of a party," "My son is in a group chat that turned cruel," "Another parent just called me about my kid," "The school isn't acting — now what." These are the app's seed content and the SEO front door for the eventual multi-school phase.

### 10.2 Ongoing channels

Communication is deliberately light: the year's calendar published early, each event announced and reminded, the occasional short survey (including the annual parent climate survey), and optionally a recorded version of the formation talks for parents who miss a session. There is no newsletter and no content channel — the content library carries the teaching, and sustained editorial channels are how volunteer efforts burn out. Resist the temptation to build more communication than the founding team can sustain.

## 11. The App

The app is a delivery and coordination layer, not the product — the covenant community is the product. It should not gate the pilot; paper and a simple website can run year one. One rule governs everything: content out, never chatter in. There is no messaging anywhere in the platform — the Project will not build for adults the medium it trains children to resist (see Section 12). Given in-house development expertise, the sensible sequence:

### Phase 1 — Content and covenant (build during pilot year)

- Covenant signing with the grade-level activation mechanic (private until threshold, then mutual introduction — the single highest-value feature to automate).
- Content library with age-of-child personalization, and event announcements/notifications.
- The Digital Oversight guide and recommended-tools shortlist, delivered as content — the enforcement tooling itself lives in separate dedicated products, not in this app.
- Grade and homeroom rosters (opt-in), event RSVPs, and formation-session recordings.
- The children's program companion app (already begun as a separate LMS-style app): curriculum tracks, milestone badges, meeting companion content. Decide during the pilot whether it remains a second app or merges into one family platform; either way, child accounts are parent-managed, data-minimal, and COPPA-compliant by design.

### Phase 2 — Coordination (year two)

- Facilitation requests: a quiet, structured way to request a facilitated conversation without a dozen awkward texts.
- Formation-series progress for parents; discussion guides in-app for Leads, Hosts, and small-group leaders.
- Multi-school support: any parent can open their school, content is white-labeled, activation mechanics are per-school-per-grade.

### What the app must never do

No incident reporting, no logging of conflicts, no naming of children in any stored record, no anonymous accusation features, and no discussion forums (which become the gossip network the Project exists to prevent). These exclusions are principled and also prudent: a database of allegations about named minors is a defamation, privacy, and safeguarding liability that would end the ministry. The app carries content, commitments, and coordination — never cases.

## 12. Stewardship and Guardrails

How the organization operates — entity structure, licensing, terms of use, safety guidance, and sustainability — lives in a companion document, [Stewardship & Operations](stewardship-and-operations.md), so that this plan stays about the mission. What belongs here is the short version and the guardrails that shape daily culture.

**The short version.** The organization publishes a free framework and stewards its content, name, and app; the local groups that adopt it — Savio Communities — are independent, self-organizing parent groups responsible for their own activities. [Steward Entity] serves as steward of the Project's intellectual property until a dedicated entity is formed, under a published [Stewardship Commitment](stewardship-commitment.md) whose first promise is that the mission is free for families, forever.

Standing guardrails, adopted at founding and printed in every manual: children are never named in written or digital records; facilitators never adjudicate and never carry information out of the room; the Project never publicly criticizes the school or parish; no Project channel may be used to discuss a specific family's situation; no Project platform carries messaging or user-generated content — the Project will not build for parents the very medium it trains children to resist; safeguarding escalations (Section 8.5) override every confidentiality norm; and leadership roles rotate on two-year terms.

## 13. Measurement

The Project measures itself annually against the death spiral it exists to reverse. The parent climate survey (anonymous, ten minutes, run at launch and each spring) is the primary instrument.

| Measure | Year-one target |
|---|---|
| Covenant signatures | ≥40% of school families; activation (8+) in at least 6 of 9 grades |
| Formation attendance | ≥50 unique parents across the series; sessions 5 and 6 the largest |
| Parent climate survey | Improvement on: "I believe other parents here would listen if I raised a concern about their child" and "My child has adults and peers who would stand up for them" |
| Protocol usage | Direct conversations reported as "went better than expected" by both sides in >60% of cases; ≥2 facilitated conversations completed with agreements kept |
| Retention | Zero covenant families withdrawing from the school citing culture (the ultimate metric, tracked over 3 years) |

## 14. Risks, Objections, and Responses

**"This is a clique of parents judging everyone else."** The covenant judges no one; it binds only its signers, and its second commitment is an admission of one's own child's capacity for harm. Structurally, roles are bounded but membership never is: there is no group to join, every gathering is open to the entire homeroom or grade, and invitations always go to all (Pillar 1). Recruitment leads with humility, never grievance.

**It becomes a gossip network.** The single most likely failure mode. Countered structurally: confidentiality norms in the covenant, no forums in the app, no case records anywhere, facilitator discretion rules, and a founding-team culture of shutting down speculation about specific families on the spot.

**Administration reacts with hostility.** Expected initially. The Project gives them nothing to object to: off-campus, opt-in, never critical of the school, and its visible outputs (kinder kids, calmer parents, fewer conflicts landing on the office desk, healthier digital habits) make their jobs easier. The courtesy briefing to the pastor frames it as parents doing their catechetical job. If hostility persists, it changes nothing — that independence is the design.

**The aggressive families never join.** They don't need to. The strategy targets the audience, not the aggressor: when 40% of families share language and standards and their children are trained upstanders, aggression loses its payoff. Some aggressive families will join late, when the covenant becomes the school's visible social norm; some never will.

**A facilitated conversation goes badly wrong.** Facilitators are trained, operate in pairs for hot cases, refuse cases beyond their scope, and never handle safeguarding matters. A written scope document protects both the facilitator and the Project.

**Legal exposure.** One bright line: the organization publishes a framework; independent local parent groups run their own communities. Terms of use, indemnification, licensing, entity structure, and safety guidance are specified in [Stewardship & Operations](stewardship-and-operations.md).

**Founder burnout / hero dependence.** Six defined volunteer roles, two-year term rotation, everything documented for handoff, and content written once for reuse. The pilot's success test includes "could this run without its founder" by year three.

**Parents sign and do nothing.** Activation mechanics (nothing activates alone), homeroom and grade gatherings that meet in person, and the formation series keep the covenant a live practice rather than a signed PDF. Annual renewal makes commitment a recurring choice.

## 15. Evidence Base and Source Material

Key sources informing this plan, for the content team and for credibility with skeptical parents:

- **Wait Until 8th** (waituntil8th.org) — 147,000+ families, 58,000+ schools, parent-to-parent spread without institutional authority. The Project borrows its pledge-activation mechanic, not its digital policy; families who take the pledge remain fully compatible with the covenant.
- **Jonathan Haidt, *The Anxious Generation*** (2024) and anxiousgeneration.com — the research case linking phone-based childhood to the mental-health and social-aggression surge; by 2026, nine states restricting school phone use. The Project shares the diagnosis while taking an oversight-first rather than blanket-delay approach (Pillar 6).
- **Lynne Lang, *Virtue-Based Restorative Discipline*** (Archdiocese of St. Louis) — Catholic restorative model; 86% of fidelity schools reported reduced bullying; source for the facilitation method and "form the adults first" principle.
- **Rachel Simmons, *Odd Girl Out*** (2002, rev. 2011) and **Rosalind Wiseman, *Queen Bees and Wannabes*** (2003) — the relational-aggression canon for the formation series; underlying research by Nicki Crick.
- **Bystander-intervention research** — reviews of school-based programs (2007–2018) found 9 of 10 improved bystander efficacy/intent and 7 of 9 improved actual behavior; grounds Pillar 5.
- **Catechism of the Catholic Church 2221–2231; Vatican II, *Gravissimum Educationis*** — parents as primary educators; the theological warrant for independence.
- **St. John Bosco, the preventive system** (reason, religion, loving-kindness) and the Oratory model; **St. Dominic Savio** — Bosco's pupil who organized peers to defend the youngest boys; patrons and method for the Project and its children's program. (The Project is a lay movement with no affiliation to the Salesian Society.)
- **Openlight Media, Disciple of Christ — Education in Virtue** — Dominican virtue curriculum with a whole-family component; a model (and possible licensed content source) for the virtue vocabulary.
